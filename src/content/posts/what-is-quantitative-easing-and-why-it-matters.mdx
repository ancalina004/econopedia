## The Basics of Quantitative Easing

When interest rates hit zero and the economy still needs a boost, central banks reach for an unconventional tool: **quantitative easing** (QE).

In simple terms, QE is when a central bank creates new money electronically and uses it to buy government bonds and other financial assets from commercial banks.

### How Does QE Work?

1. **The central bank creates money** — not by printing physical notes, but by crediting its own account digitally
2. **It buys bonds from banks** — this pushes bond prices up and yields down
3. **Banks have more reserves** — which (in theory) encourages them to lend more
4. **Borrowing becomes cheaper** — stimulating spending and investment

> "QE is essentially the central bank saying: we'll do whatever it takes to keep the economy moving."

### Why Should You Care?

QE has real consequences for everyday people:

- **Savers lose out** — lower interest rates mean less return on savings accounts
- **Asset prices rise** — property and stock markets tend to benefit
- **Currency weakens** — making imports more expensive
- **Inequality can widen** — those who own assets benefit most

### The UK Experience

The Bank of England launched its first QE programme in 2009, eventually purchasing over £895 billion in assets. While it helped stabilise the economy after the financial crisis, critics argue it inflated house prices and widened the wealth gap.

### The Bottom Line

QE is a powerful but blunt instrument. Understanding how it works helps you make better decisions about saving, investing, and planning for the future.
