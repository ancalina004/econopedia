## The Price Mechanism

Every time you buy a coffee, fill up your car, or check the price of Bitcoin, you're interacting with the forces of **supply and demand**. It's the most fundamental concept in economics.

### What Is Demand?

Demand is the quantity of a good or service that consumers are willing and able to buy at a given price. As price falls, demand typically rises — this is the **law of demand**.

### What Is Supply?

Supply is the quantity that producers are willing and able to sell at a given price. As price rises, supply typically increases — the **law of supply**.

### Market Equilibrium

When supply equals demand, we reach **equilibrium** — the price at which the market clears. No surplus, no shortage.

> "The market price is where the plans of buyers and sellers align."

### Shifts vs Movements

A common exam mistake: confusing **movements along** a curve with **shifts of** the curve.

- **Movement**: caused by a change in the good's own price
- **Shift**: caused by a change in another factor (income, tastes, costs)

#### Factors That Shift Demand
1. Changes in income
2. Price of substitutes or complements
3. Consumer preferences
4. Population changes

#### Factors That Shift Supply
1. Input costs (wages, raw materials)
2. Technology improvements
3. Government taxes or subsidies
4. Number of firms in the market

### Real-World Example

When a drought reduces coffee bean harvests, the supply curve shifts left. With demand unchanged, the equilibrium price rises — which is why your morning latte gets more expensive.

Understanding these mechanics gives you a framework for analysing almost any market.
